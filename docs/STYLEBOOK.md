# Stylebook

Vital City's working stylebook. Except where noted, follow AP style, using the Merriam-Webster online dictionary for spelling and definitions.

When items are not covered below or in AP, consult (in order): Chicago Manual of Style, Strunk and White, New York Times Stylebook, and — if applicable — a professional or academic publishing guide (AMA, APA, MLA). For neologisms or internet terms not covered, consult the BuzzFeed Style Guide. For inclusive language, consult the GLAAD Media Reference Guide, then the Conscious Style Guide.

---

## Punctuation

**Dashes.** Em dashes with spaces on both sides. Do not use double hyphens in place of an em dash. Do not use en dashes. For attributions following block quotes or epigraphs, use an em dash followed by a space.

**Commas.** No serial / Oxford comma, per AP. Exceptions: for clarity, when separating complete or complex phrases, or when preceding "etc." In general, commas are not needed except for appositives or joining two complete or complex phrases (especially those joined with a conjunction).

**Colons.** Capitalize full sentences, questions and commands following a colon.

**Slashes (contra AP).** When a slash separates two or more single-word terms, no spaces on either side (`and/but/or`). When one or more terms consist of multiple words, surround the slash with single spaces (`World War I / World War II`, `monthly / year-end`).

**Periods.** Not used in headlines (generally). Used for deks that are complete sentences. For headers/subheaders in Data Story pieces, complete sentences do not take a period unless multiple sentences appear together.

**Ellipsis.** Use the single-character ellipsis (`…`) in lieu of three unspaced periods.

## Capitalization

- **Titles** in title case (contra AP). Capitalize "to" when part of an infinitive. Capitalize each word in phrasal verbs and adverbs. Follow a composition's own capitalization when citing its name.
- **Deks** in sentence case.
- **Capitalize** full sentences, questions and commands following a colon.

## Numbers

- Spell out one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above.
- **Always numerals:** ages, ratios, sequential designations (Act 3, Exit 2, Game 3, Phase 1, Stage 3 cancer, Category 4 storm, Type 2 diabetes), percentages, decimals, currency, mathematical uses, numbers in millions.
- **Always spelled out:** fractions less than 1 (two-thirds, one-half), issue numbers (Issue Three).
- **Singular number descriptors** are capitalized (Game 4); **plural** are lower case (games 4 and 5).
- **Never** start a sentence with a numeral except for years.
- **Year ranges:** use all four digits on both ends (1998-1999, not 1998-99).
- **Use %, not percent**, per AP.
- **No need to standardize** numeral styles within a sentence (three hot dogs and 11 hamburgers).

## Data

`data` is treated as a **plural** noun, contra AP. `dataset` is one word.

## Dates, fiscal years

- Do not capitalize `fiscal year`. Abbreviate as `FY YYYY` (space, four digits). `FY YY` is acceptable in charts when there's no risk of confusion.

## Academic degrees

Use periods when abbreviated: B.A., M.A., M.D., Ph.D. Exceptions: MBA, MPA, MPH, MFA, MSW.

Written out: `bachelor's degree`, `master's degree` (lower case, possessive with apostrophe); `Bachelor of Science`, `Master of Business Administration` (capitalize, not possessive). `a bachelor's [degree] in psychology`.

## Race, ethnicity and U.S. race issues

- `Black` — capitalize. `African American` — capitalize, no hyphen.
- `brown` — do not capitalize when referring to race(s) of groups of people.
- It is acceptable, but not ideal, to use adjectives as nouns: `whites`, `Blacks`, `Afro-Caribbeans`.
- Avoid `George Floyd protests` and similar constructions that would make the victim appear to have been provoking protest. Make clear what protests are for or against: "protests following the murder of George Floyd", "Black Lives Matter protests".

## Historical events

Capitalize names of key historical events, eras, wars, etc., and do not use quotation marks: the Great Depression, the Renaissance, the Arab Spring, the Civil Rights Movement, the Cold War, the New Deal, Occupy Wall Street.

Notable exceptions: `the war on drugs`, `the war on terror` — neither is an official designator and both have referred to multiple or ongoing periods. Generic-name events take lower case except proper nouns: `the war in Afghanistan`, `the Kennedy assassination`, `the stock market crash of 1929`.

## New York-specific

### "City" and "State"

- **Capitalize** `City` / `State` when referring to New York City or State as a governing or administrative body. Lower case when referring to the population or geographic entity. (Contra AP.)
  - "When the City implemented a citywide policy change, New Yorkers all across the city responded positively."
  - "When the city implemented a citywide policy change, St. Louisans all across the city responded positively."
- **New York City** — spell out on first reference unless part of a proper name (NYC Pandemic Response Institute). "New York" acceptable on second reference. "NYC" acceptable in chart footnotes; otherwise avoid.

### Neighborhoods

- `the Bronx` (lowercase "the")
- `Midtown` (neighborhood) / `midtown Manhattan` (geography)
- `NoMad`, `NoHo`, `SoHo`, `TriBeCa` but `Dumbo`
- `Prospect Lefferts Gardens` (no hyphen)
- `downtown` / `Downtown`, `midtown` / `Midtown`, `uptown` / `Uptown` — lowercase when used as adjective or generic noun; upper case when referring to a specific neighborhood.

### City Council

Capitalize when part of a proper name (`New York City Council`, `City Council`). For New York City Council specifically, retain the capitalization when it appears alone: "This year, the Council enacted major policy changes." (contra AP). `council members` — two words for New York City.

### City Hall

Capitalize when referring to a specific city government or specific building.

### Transit

- Lowercase `subway`, `bus`, `train` unless part of the formal system name (`New York City Subway`, but `the Second Avenue subway`).
- Running text: `the A train`, `the 5 train`, `a Jamaica-bound E train`, `the QM10 express bus`.
- Multiple same-path lines: slash-separated — `the 4/5 trains`, `the A/C/E`.
- LIRR, Metro-North, NJ Transit, PATH: use line or branch name as written (`Babylon Branch`, `Hudson Line`, `Hudson-Bergen Light Rail`).
- **Station.** Lowercase when preceded by an article (`the 23rd Street station`). Capitalize when used singularly (`59th Street-Columbus Circle Station`). Join hyphenated station names with no spaces. Spell out "Avenue", "Street", etc. Exceptions: `St.` (for "Saint"), airport abbreviations (`JFK`), `Penn Station`.
- Use numerals for numbered street stations (`West 4th Street`).
- Spell out ordinals first through ninth for line names (`Second Avenue Subway`, `Sixth Avenue Line`).
- Capitalize `Express` and `Local` when part of a formal name.
- `AirTrain` — note caps.
- `Metropolitan Transportation Authority` / `MTA` — spell out on first use; `Transportation`, not `Transit`.
- `mass transit` — no hyphen when used as a compound modifier.

## Crime / criminal justice

- `criminal legal system` preferred; follow author's preference.
- `correction officer` (per NYC/NYS); `correctional staff` acceptable. For names of departments, follow the entity's preferred style (`New York City Department of Correction`, `Pennsylvania Department of Corrections`).
- `Corrections` (lower case) when referring to the concept.
- `CompStat`, `NeighborhoodStat` — note caps (not `COMPSTAT`, not `Compstat`).
- `gang database` — not capitalized when referring to the NYPD's Criminal Group Database. Place in quotation marks on first reference. `Criminal Group Database` — capitalized as the formal tool name.
- `crack cocaine epidemic` (not `crack epidemic`, per AP), lower case, no quotation marks.
- `stop-and-frisk` — no quotes, hyphenated when noun or adjective, no hyphen when verb phrase.
- `precision policing` — never hyphenated.
- `broken windows` / `broken windows theory` — no caps, no quotation marks (unless in a title or quote). No hyphen as adjective.
- `plantation model` — lower case, no hyphen as adjective.

## Housing, planning, development

- `real estate` — never hyphenated, noun or adjective.
- `rent burden` (noun, space). Hyphenated as a compound adjective (`rent-burden figures`). `rent-burdened` — always hyphenated.
- `floor-area ratio` — hyphenate "floor-area". As an adjective: `floor-area-ratio`.
- `co-living` — hyphenate.
- `live-work` — hyphenate (abbreviating "live-work space").
- `5-over-1` — always numerals, always hyphenated. "Five-over-one" at the beginning of a sentence. Same for all similar constructions.
- `open-gangway` — hyphenate as compound modifier.
- `point access` — never hyphenated (building/block concept).
- `sightlines` — one word (architectural concept).
- `state of good repair` — no caps, no quotes (transit concept).
- `city-making` — hyphenate in all instances.
- `outer-borough` / `outer borough` — hyphenate as adjective, open as noun.
- `interborough`, `intraborough` — closed, noun or adjective.
- `tri-state` — not `tristate`. Do not capitalize "tri-state area".
- `zoning` — `dezone`, `downzone`, `upzone`, `rezone` — no hyphens.
- `wayfinding` — one word, no hyphen.

## Health, substance use

- `substance use disorder` — no hyphen in "substance use".
- `dope sick` — two words.
- `drunk driving` — per M-W, contra AP.
- `health care` — two words.
- `childcare` — one word (per M-W).
- `eldercare` — one word (per M-W).
- `day care` — two words.
- `foster care` — two words, never hyphenated.
- `self-care` — always hyphenated.
- `preventive` — not "preventative".

## Policy, government

- `policymaker`, `policymaking` — no hyphen (per AP and M-W).
- `decision-maker`, `decision-making` — with hyphen, for clarity.
- `grantmaker`, `grantmaking` — no hyphen (per AP on philanthropy).
- `nonprofit` — no hyphen. Not interchangeable with `not-for-profit`, which is not a legal designation.
- `anti-poverty` — hyphenate (per M-W).
- `postpandemic`, `prepandemic` — no hyphen (per AP). Same for `postriot`.
- `work-from-home` — hyphenate as noun or adjective; no quotation marks.
- `high net worth` / `high-net-worth` — double hyphenate as phrasal adjective before a noun (per NYT/WSJ).
- `class action` (noun) / `class-action` (adjective, e.g., "a class-action lawsuit").
- `long-term` — hyphenate. `longtime` — close up (per M-W).
- `before and after` (noun) / `before-and-after` (adjective).

## Common words

- `acknowledgment` — note spelling (per AP).
- `advisor` — not `adviser` (contra AP).
- `alt-weekly` — hyphenate.
- `analog` — not `analogue` (per AP).
- `average Joe` — lowercase a, capital J.
- `bodega` — no need to define on first instance.
- `byzantine` — lower case when used to mean "complicated"; initial cap when referring to the Church or empire.
- `cafe` — no accent.
- `cellphone` — one word (per AP, contra M-W).
- `centuries` — styled as "20th century" in all cases. Digits, no capital C, no hyphen (including adjectivally). Exceptions: sentence-starting "Twentieth century" (still no cap C or hyphen), and "first century" through "ninth century".
- `civil rights` — never hyphenated.
- `fare beating` — two words.
- `gray` — not "grey" (per AP).
- `Great Recession` — capitalize (2007-2009).
- `gun control`, `gun violence` — never hyphenated.
- `hood` — as slang for neighborhood, no definition needed. No apostrophe.
- `hot spot` (noun) / `hot-spot` (adjective).
- `ID'd` — past tense of the verb ID. Not `IDed` or `IDd`.
- `judgment-proof` — hyphenate in all cases. `judgment proofness` — no hyphen (two nouns, per Chicago).
- `naive` — no umlaut.
- `outsize` — not `outsized` (per M-W).
- `petit` for `petit theft`, `petit larceny`, `petit jury`, `petit cash`. `petty` for everything else.
- `poverty maintenance` — never hyphenated.
- `R&D` — no spaces (contra M-W).
- `rideshare` (noun) — closed. `ride-sharing` — hyphenated (per M-W and AP).
- `superexpress` — one word.
- `think piece` — two words.
- `time frame` — two words.
- `urban doom loop` — no caps in running text. Do not hyphenate as a compound adjective. Acceptable on first use; `doom loop` on subsequent uses. Quotes OK on first use or for context.
- `U.S.` — periods, except in headlines (`US`).
- `War on Poverty` — capitalize, no quotation marks. `war on drugs` — lower case, no quotation marks.

## Correlative conjunctions

No comma, per AP, except for clarity or when joining independent clauses. Common forms: `not/but`, `if/then`, `both/and`, `whether/or`, `neither/nor`, `either/or`.

- "It is prevalent not only in New York but in all major U.S. cities."
- "The project will be complete if not today then tomorrow."
- "Both she and I must participate."
- **But:** "If we focus on enhancing our built environment, then we can not only improve our quality of life, reduce crime and create a more beautiful city, but we can also reduce our carbon footprint."

## `from … to`

No comma unless for clarity or separating lists.

- "From one to another to yet another …"
- "From vanilla to chocolate, from rum raisin to rocky road, every ice cream flavor has a die-hard fan."

## Collective nouns

Take singular verbs and pronouns. Collective proper nouns agree with the noun: `The Cardinals are playing the Mets tonight; Microsoft is releasing new software.`

## Accents and diacriticals

Do not use except as part of a person's name, a preferred word for a person's identity, or in a direct quotation outside common parlance: `cafe`, `fiance`, `facade`, but `Chloë`, `Métis`, `"Après moi, le déluge."`

## Italics

Do not use, per AP. Exception: quotation of written materials where removing italics may alter meaning.

## Hyperlinks

- Ideally, link more than one word.
- Avoid consecutive hyperlinks without a line break or unlinked text between.
- Do not include surrounding punctuation, with these exceptions: quotation marks (and punctuation within quotation marks), and punctuation that makes up part of a name or title.
- For academic sources, use the DOI link where possible (format: `doi.org/[DOI NUMBER]`).

## Pull quotes

Do not use quotation marks to enclose pull quotes. Pull quotes are formatted exactly as they would appear in the body of the article — caps, double quotation marks, italics, etc.

## Captions, deks

- **Deks** — sentence case. Final punctuation only if the dek is a complete sentence or a sentence fragment following a complete sentence.

## NYU

`NYU` acceptable on first reference. Also acceptable when context about discipline is clear: `NYU Wagner`, `NYU Stern`, `NYU Law`, `NYU Dentistry`, `NYU Gallatin`, `NYU Steinhardt`, `NYU Tisch`, `NYU Langone Health`.

## Professor

Capitalize before a name in running text: `Professor Lipkin` (contra AP).

## Attribution

For attributions following block quotes or epigraphs, use an em dash followed by a space.
